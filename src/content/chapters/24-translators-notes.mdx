<p class="text-center text-sm uppercase tracking-widest text-gray-500 dark:text-gray-400 mb-6">Translator's Notes</p>

<p id="T-01" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-01</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> "Stop, traveler, you tread upon a hero's dust!" Epitaph inscribed by the Count d'Enghien on the tomb of his great opponent, General Mercy, killed in 1645 in the Thirty Years' War.</p>

<p id="T-02" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-02</b> — <i>Nephilim</i>: giants, titans, fallen angels.</p>

<p id="T-03" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-03</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Aryavarta</i> — ancient name for northern India.</p>

<p id="T-07" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-07</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> The Thomist — Saint Thomas Aquinas.</p>

<p id="T-08" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-08</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Saint Ignatius of Loyola, founder of the Jesuits.</p>

<p id="T-09" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-09</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> The French original uses <i>Gigogne</i> — a French expression denoting a prolific fatherhood/motherhood.</p>

<p id="T-10" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-10</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Orthodoxy</i> — a religious doctrine held to be true.</p>

<p id="T-11" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-11</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Moxa</i> — a cotton wick that Eastern peoples apply, lit, to cauterize wounds.</p>

<p id="T-15" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-15</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Play on words in the French original: <i>"de la Mer à la Mare"</i> (from the Sea to the Pond).</p>

<p id="T-16" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-16</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Periplus</i> — coast-hugging navigation; a circular voyage.</p>

<p id="T-18" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-18</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Purgatory.</p>

<p id="T-19" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-19</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Alpha and Omega.</p>

<p id="T-21" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-21</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Richelieu and Mazarin.</p>

<p id="T-22" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-22</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Letter from Saint-Yves to Papus.</p>

<p id="T-23" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-23</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Inventory</i> — existing books.</p>

<p id="T-25" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-25</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> All the Correspondences given here refer to the Northern Hemisphere.</p>

<p id="T-26" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-26</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Under conventional astrology, Libra would be attributed to Venus as its diurnal domicile.</p>

<p id="T-27" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-27</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> This contradicts the traditional astrological assignment of Mars's diurnal domicile to Aries and nocturnal to Scorpio.</p>

<p id="T-28" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-28</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Under conventional astrology, Gemini would be considered the diurnal domicile of Mercury.</p>

<p id="T-29" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-29</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Again, Mars's domicile contradicts traditional astrology.</p>

<p id="T-30" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-30</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Under conventional astrology, Virgo is considered the nocturnal domicile of Mercury.</p>

<p id="T-31" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-31</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> A pagoda is an Oriental temple.</p>

<p id="T-32" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-32</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Massorah</i> — a critical work on the spelling and correct reading of the Hebrew Bible, compiled by Jewish doctors.</p>

<p id="T-33" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-33</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Archeometric Mantras</i> — a term coined by Saint-Yves to designate readings made on the scales of the Archeometer.</p>

<p id="T-35" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-35</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Pluto does not appear here, as it had not yet been discovered at the time this book was written.</p>

<p id="T-36" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-36</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> There appears to be a confusion here: each 30° represents a rotation of 2 hours, so the twelve 30° divisions correspond to twice that span, i.e. 24 hours; therefore one must multiply by two (2) rather than divide.</p>

<p id="T-37" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-37</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> The author claims that Uranus and Neptune's influence is insignificant compared to the closer planets.</p>

<p id="T-38" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-38</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> The entire reasoning is extraordinarily absurd and false, because the author attempts to apply to an occult science — astrology — premises drawn from a materialist science. It is not the visible material body of the planet that carries astrological influence; in any case, the astrological influence of the post-Saturnian planets has been clearly demonstrated.</p>

<p id="T-39" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-39</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Matrix</i> — the womb.</p>

<p id="T-44" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-44</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> A jar for washing the hands.</p>

<p id="T-45" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-45</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> <i>Segments</i>.</p>

<p id="T-46" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-46</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> Regarding the patent — particularly the standard and the adaptations — reproduced in full below.</p>

<p id="T-48" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-48</b> — <i>Translator's Note:</i> See the chalices above.</p>

<p id="T-49" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-49</b> — <i>Editor's Note:</i> In 19th-century French, <i>rasta</i> is common shorthand for <i>rastaquouère</i> — a flashy, suspicious social climber of dubious foreign origin. Not to be confused with the English word "Rasta" (Rastafarian).</p>

<p id="T-50" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-50</b> — <i>Editor's Note:</i> <i>Pot-Bouille</i> — a literary allusion to Émile Zola's 1882 novel of the same name, which depicts the moral decay and hidden sordidness of the Parisian bourgeoisie.</p>

<p id="T-51" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-51</b> — <i>Editor's Note:</i> <i>La lanterne des bourgeois de Falaise</i> — a French idiom meaning an explanation that explains nothing, a light that sheds no light.</p>

<p id="T-52" class="text-sm mb-3"><b>T-52</b> — <i>Editor's Note:</i> <i>Monsieur Dimanche</i> — the hapless creditor in Molière's <i>Don Juan</i> (1665), a character who is endlessly put off and manipulated by the debtor he tries to collect from.</p>
